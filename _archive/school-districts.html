How Compact Are King County's School Boundaries?

Ranking boundaries by the Polsby–Popper compactness score at two scales — 18 school districts across King County, then 14 elementary attendance zones inside one district — to show why shape alone cannot diagnose gerrymandering.

Lucas · Mentored by Prof. Bo Zhao, Humanistic GIS Lab, University of Washington

The Polsby–Popper (PP) score measures how compact a polygon is by comparing its area to the area of a circle with the same perimeter:

PP = 4πA / P2

A perfect circle scores 1.0; the more a boundary wiggles, the closer the score falls toward 0. Below, every unified school district whose center lies in King County, Washington is shaded by its PP score over a greyscale vector basemap. Red districts are the least compact; blue are the most compact.

Read this as a shape metric, not a gerrymandering detector. This research argues — following Zhao (2026) — that low compactness does not establish manipulation. A district can look irregular simply because it traces a shoreline or a lake, and it can look tidy purely because it sits on an island. The ranking here is the setup for that argument, not evidence of wrongdoing.

What the ranking actually shows

Sort the table and the pattern is revealing — and it cuts against reading PP as a fairness score:

  • The most “compact” districts are islands. Vashon Island and Mercer Island top the compactness ranking not because anyone drew them fairly, but because water defines their edges. Geography, not intent, sets the shape.
  • The least compact districts hug natural and built features. The low-scoring districts wind along rivers, lakeshores, and annexation seams — exactly the “irregular for innocent reasons” case that makes raw compactness unreliable.
  • School districts aren’t partisan-drawn at all. Their boundaries reflect enrollment, funding, and historical annexation, not the packing-and-cracking logic of partisan redistricting. A low PP here flags an interesting shape to investigate, nothing more.

This is precisely why the main study turns to Markov Chain Monte Carlo ensembles: to judge a map you have to compare it against thousands of valid alternatives, not against an idealized circle.

Scale 2 — elementary attendance zones inside Bellevue

The same compactness math is scale-invariant — it works identically whether the unit is a whole county's districts or the catchment zones inside a single district. Here we zoom into the Bellevue School District and rank its 14 elementary attendance zones, the areas that decide which neighborhood feeds which elementary school, by the identical Polsby–Popper score and the same color scale as above.

Why this scale matters. Attendance-zone lines are where boundary drawing most directly shapes who goes to school with whom — the segregation-and-funding equity question, not partisan packing. And the same caveat holds: a wiggly zone may simply trace Lake Washington, I-90, or a hillside, so a low compactness score flags a shape to examine, never proof of intent.

Methods & data

  • District boundaries: U.S. Census Bureau TIGERweb, current unified school districts, filtered to those centered within King County.
  • Bellevue attendance zones: City of Bellevue GIS feature service, “Attendance Areas (Elementary School)” (14 zones). Built by data/build_bellevue_elementary.py.
  • Compactness: PP = 4πA/P2, computed on the sphere — spherical-excess area and haversine (geodesic) perimeter — so the score is projection-correct rather than distorted by raw longitude/latitude degrees.
  • Reproducibility: the two build scripts re-download from source and regenerate data/school_districts.geojson and data/bellevue_elementary.geojson.
