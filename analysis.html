What the 2023 rezoning did — a guided reading

This page walks through the evidence in order. Every claim links to the interactive view where you can check it yourself. Numbers describe; the judging is yours.

Section 1

How to read this page

There are three ways to look at one boundary change, and this page uses all three:

  • The shape of the zones. Did the lines themselves get more or less regular? This is the lens the public reaches for first — and the one we examine most carefully in Section 4.
  • The flows. Which territory, students, and programs moved where? This is the Boundary changes page's job.
  • The human effects. What changed about travel, capacity, demographics, and access to programs, for the district and for each zone? This is Section 3, using the Map tool's measurements.

One promise, repeated from the front door: no number on this site is a fairness score. We measured what can be measured and describe what cannot. Whether any of it was fair is a judgment that belongs to the people affected — this page just makes sure that judgment can be an informed one.

Section 2

What happened

In 2018, the district opened Wilburton Elementary — a new school built to relieve elementary overcrowding — and assembled a zone for it from pieces of neighboring zones. Five years later the picture had reversed: elementary enrollment was falling, and the district projected a budget shortfall. By 2022–23, Wilburton's 650-seat building held about 350 students; Eastgate's 529 seats held about 316.

In 2023, the school board voted to close both schools. Their territory was reassigned to three neighbors — Spiritridge, Enatai, and Clyde Hill — and programs moved with the students: Advanced Learning left Spiritridge for Woodridge, and Eastgate's Olympic program moved to Spiritridge. In total, about 13.6 km² of the district — home to roughly — switched elementary schools.

Enrollment figures are the district's own counts; the falling-enrollment and budget rationale is the district's stated reason for the consolidation, reported here as their statement.10,14 Population estimates use the fixed 2020 census snapshot.

Section 3

The impacts, one by one

Each card below starts with the question a family would actually ask, then answers with before/after measurements. The small tag on each card says how far the numbers go: measured means the answer is a computation, partly measured means numbers cover only part of the question, and described means honest words are all the data allows.

Travel — did school get harder to reach?

measured

We routed the real road network from every home to its assigned school — driving and walking — and averaged by where school-age children live. For most of the district the answer is simply no change: 11 of the 16 zones kept identical boundaries in 2023. For the reassigned areas, the answer is a real cost:

The two units tell different stories, which is why the toggle exists: by car these changes look modest; on foot they are the difference between a school you can walk to and one you cannot. Zone-wide, Spiritridge — which absorbed the Eastgate territory — saw its average drive rise from 4.0 to 7.5 minutes. That sounds small until you say it in walking terms: the same average trip went from a 19-minute walk to a 41-minute walk — from "kids can walk" to "somebody drives, twice a day, every day."

Demographic mix — did the lines sort children apart?

measured

First, the idea in plain words. Bellevue's families are not spread evenly across the city — different neighborhoods have different mixes of White, Asian, Hispanic, and Black residents. When attendance lines are drawn on top of that, each zone inherits a mix. "Sorting" is the question of how different the zones' mixes end up being from one another: if every zone looked just like the district as a whole, there would be no sorting at all. One design choice to know: every era here uses the same frozen 2020 census population. Real families did move during these years, but we deliberately hold the population still, so that any difference you see between eras isolates what the line-drawing alone did — a fair before/after comparison needs everything else held constant.

Each zone gets a mix gap — how far its mix sits from the district's overall mix. The scale: 0 means identical to the district, and the most distinctive zone in Bellevue (Stevenson) scores about 0.07. So when Spiritridge went from 0.036 to 0.010, it went from being one of the more distinctive zones in the district to nearly indistinguishable from the district average — on this scale, that is a large move.

The result many readers won't expect: the 2023 consolidation slightly reduced sorting. The district-wide index eased from 0.031 to 0.028 (about a 10% drop), every rezoned zone moved closer to the district's mix (Enatai 0.024 → 0.006, Clyde Hill 0.012 → 0.008), and the 2018 rezoning had barely moved it either way (0.030 → 0.031). Merging territories tends to average out their differences; whatever else the closures did, they did not carve out demographically extreme zones.

One more distinction worth knowing, using the standard dissimilarity index — roughly, out of every 100 children, how many would have to switch schools for every school to mirror the district's mix (0 = none, 100 = total separation). Measured on who lives where, today's zones score about 15 of every 100. Measured on who actually enrolls where, it's about 19 of every 100 — schools sort more than neighborhoods do, because choice schools and special programs pull the enrolled mix further apart than residence alone would.

Indices depend on which groups are counted (we use six: White, Asian, Hispanic, Black, two-or-more, other) and describe sorting, not intent and not income. Low sorting is not automatically "good," nor high sorting "bad" — the numbers only say how alike the zones are.

Program access — can families still reach optional programs?

partly measured

Some programs live at specific schools, so when schools close or programs move, the distance to the nearest host changes even for families whose own school didn't. In 2023: Advanced Learning moved from Spiritridge to Woodridge, Eastgate's Olympic program moved to Spiritridge, and Jing Mei (Mandarin immersion) moved into the former Wilburton building. A Japanese immersion strand at Bennett was added after 2023.

District-wide, the average distance to the nearest host barely moved — Advanced Learning , and similarly small shifts for immersion and site-based special education. But the average hides the local swings, which ran in opposite directions:

  • Woodridge residents' distance to Advanced Learning — the program came to them.
  • Spiritridge residents' distance to it — the program left them.
  • The old Eastgate territory's distance to site-based special education — the Olympic program had been at Eastgate itself.

Why only "partly measured": distance answers can you get there, not can you get in — eligibility, admission to competitive programs, and available seats are not on any map. Pre-2018 program locations are too thinly documented to compare, so this card covers the 2023 change only.

Special education — how far are the site-based services?

partly measured

This gets its own card, separate from optional programs, because special-education services are a legal right, not a choice. Every school serves students with IEPs day to day; what lives at specific sites are the district's three named intensive programs — Olympic (autism spectrum), Pacific (developmental and intellectual disabilities), and Cascade (social/emotional/behavioral). When a host site closes, the map of those programs changes.

Neighborhood continuity — did the lines keep communities together?

partly measured

A neighborhood counts as "split" when its children are zoned to different elementary schools — classmates on one street, different schools on the next. First, the yardstick, because it surprises people: splits are the norm here. Of Bellevue's 16 official city neighborhoods with school-age children, 13 are split across two or more elementary zones today. School lines and neighborhood lines were never the same map.

Against that baseline, the 2023 consolidation changed remarkably little — but what it changed is pointed. Exactly two neighborhoods' split counts moved: the Wilburton neighborhood went from whole to split (its children had all attended one school; they now divide between Clyde Hill and Enatai), and Eastgate became less split (from four schools to three). The neighborhood that lost its school also lost its single shared school community — that is the human reading of "1 → 2."

Counts cover the City of Bellevue's official neighborhoods only (the district also serves Clyde Hill, Medina, and other towns that have no Bellevue neighborhood), and a split count is not a measure of community feeling — it counts assignments, not friendships. Where a neighborhood extends outside the school district entirely, that portion does not count as an extra school in its split count: those children were never Bellevue's to assign, so counting them would blame the district's lines for a boundary the district doesn't control. The outside share is listed separately in each neighborhood's popup on the map tool. One preview of Section 4: a boundary that follows a neighborhood edge keeps the community whole but often makes the zone's shape score worse — the shape metric punishes the community-friendly line.

Housing — who can afford to live in each zone?

partly measured

School zones are also price tags: to enroll at a school by right, you live inside its line, so each zone's housing costs decide who can join it. The scale in Bellevue is its own yardstick: zone median home values run from about $0.8M (Ardmore) to $1.8M (Medina) — all but two zones sit above a million dollars — with zone median household incomes from about $124,000 (Stevenson) to $201,000 (Cherry Crest).

Because all eras use the same frozen household data, redrawing lines changes a zone's housing profile only by regrouping homes — and 2023 did visibly regroup them. Clyde Hill's zone, which absorbed part of the Wilburton area with its apartments and denser housing, saw its median income figure move from about $198,000 to $186,000 and its median home value from $1.66M to $1.48M — the zone now spans a much wider range of housing than before. Spiritridge's median income figure rose from about $170,000 to $182,000. Untouched zones didn't move a dollar — same households, same lines.

Dollar figures are American Community Survey 2019–2023 estimates (the census's household survey), apportioned to zones by where people lived — they say who lived where, not what the rezoning did to prices. Whether school assignments move home prices ("capitalization") is a real research question we deliberately do not claim here; the map tool's price-history charts are context, not attribution.

Language — where do multilingual families live?

partly measured

The census calls a household "limited-English" when no one 14 or older speaks English very well — the households for whom school letters, meetings, and deadlines are hardest to navigate. District-wide, about 9% of households qualify, but they are not spread evenly: today's zones range from 2.5% of households (Medina) to 13.1% (Somerset) — a five-fold difference between zones in the same district.

Measured as shares, the 2023 lines barely moved this map: Clyde Hill went 11.7% → 11.3%, Enatai 8.5% → 9.1%, Spiritridge 5.5% → 5.2%. Measured as counts — flip the toggle — the change is real: the closed Wilburton zone held one of the district's largest multilingual populations, roughly 970 limited-English households, and responsibility for them moved to Clyde Hill and Enatai, whose zones now carry the district's two largest limited-English counts (about 1,020 and 940 households). The percentages stayed flat only because the receiving zones were already large and already multilingual.

Why "partly measured": where multilingual families live is measurable; whether they could effectively follow and respond to the consolidation process (translated materials, meeting formats, timelines) is a process question — it belongs to Section 6. Dual-language programs are covered in the program-access card above.

Middle & high schools — the ripple nobody rezoned

partly measured

Here is a finding that looks like nothing: middle and high school boundaries did not change at all — not in 2018, not in 2023. Only elementary lines moved. You can verify the "nothing" yourself on the map tool.

But unchanged lines do not mean unchanged schools, because of feeder patterns — which elementary cohorts arrive together at each middle school. A middle school's entering class is built from the elementary communities below it, and 2023 rebuilt some of those communities: children from the old Eastgate zone now arrive having spent elementary years at Spiritridge instead of Eastgate. Same building, same boundary, a differently-woven entering class.

The structural tension was there before 2023 and remains: elementary zones do not nest neatly inside middle zones. Phantom Lake's children split roughly 54/46 between two middle schools, and Stevenson's split at both the middle and high level — families there face a built-in choice between keeping the elementary community together and attending the nearest school. The 2023 change didn't create that tension, but every rezoning reshuffles who experiences it.

"Partly measured": the boundary stability and the feeder splits are computed; how a re-woven cohort feels to the students in it is not a number.

Stability — how often do the same streets get rezoned?

partly measured

A boundary map can look reasonable in any single year and still be hard to live under, if it keeps changing. The yardstick here is a child's own timeline: elementary school lasts six years, so two rezonings five years apart can both land on the same student.

That is exactly what happened in the center of this story. The Wilburton area was rezoned in 2018 (carved out of Woodridge, Enatai, and Clyde Hill for the brand-new school) and rezoned again in 2023 (handed to Clyde Hill and Enatai when that school closed). A child who started kindergarten at Wilburton when it opened attended a school that no longer existed by the time they left fifth grade.2 Enatai and Clyde Hill ran the loop in reverse: they gave territory to Wilburton in 2018 and received it — plus more — back in 2023.

The count across our three eras: most of the district was rezoned zero times — the majority of zones are identical in all three maps. A handful of areas moved once. And one area — about 8.5 km², home to roughly 1,200 children — was rezoned twice. Instability was not spread around; it was concentrated on the same streets, both times.

Why "partly measured": how often each area was rezoned is a count (zero, once, or twice, above); what repeated rezoning does to a child's friendships and a family's plans is not a number. The 2018 boundaries come from a different (federal) source, so the 2018 pieces are close estimates; the 2023 pieces are exact. And whether the 2018–2023 whiplash was avoidable planning error or a reasonable response to enrollment that genuinely reversed is a judgment we leave open — the enrollment numbers are in the capacity card.

Participation — who was heard before the decision?

described

Every impact above asks what the decision did. This one asks how the decision was made — and it is the impact our numbers can say the least about, which is itself worth being honest about. On paper, the process was uniform: the same public meetings, the same comment channels, open to every family in the district. The real question is whether effective voice was uniform — whether a family working two jobs, or navigating the process in a second language, had the same practical chance to be heard as a family with time, English, and experience with school politics.

The record in brief: about eight weeks passed between the seven-school candidate list (mid-January 2023) and the board's 3–2-abstention vote (March 16).2,3 Formal channels existed at every step — school open houses, board hearings, a final comment night, review committees. The contested part is access: every key meeting ran at 4:30 p.m. on a weekday at one central building — a spot our routing places near the district's drive-time center (every corner of the district is within ~18 minutes by car), so the barrier was the hour, not the distance;11 the PTAs' joint letter said the proposal wasn't translated for multilingual families;9 parents in the boardroom learned the recommendation from a TV press release;3 and Eastgate's Chinese-immigrant families ran their own bilingual flyer campaign in the final week, saying official communication hadn't reached them.3

Input did move parts of the plan: Ardmore came off the list — the interim superintendent credited "the request of parents to give them a chance"12 — and the committees appeared after the PTAs asked why none existed.9,11 The structural decisions and the timeline never moved. Section 4

What shape can — and cannot — see

When a redistricting fight makes the news, one argument always shows up first: look how weird the map is. The instinct behind it — strange shape means someone engineered it — is the most trusted tool the public has for judging boundaries. This project began with that tool, so this section takes it seriously instead of dismissing it: what can a shape score actually tell you about what happened to families in 2023?

First, the honest limit: the formula is blind

Our main shape score, Polsby–Popper, compares a zone's area to its perimeter: a perfect circle scores 1, and the stringier the outline, the closer to 0. Bellevue's elementary zones run from about 0.25 to 0.65. Notice what goes into that formula: area and perimeter. No person, price, program, or travel time appears anywhere in it. By construction, a shape score cannot measure a single one of the eleven impacts in Section 3 — not because it's a bad formula, but because it's answering a different question than people think it is.

Second, the honest credit: shape still correlates

And yet the public instinct isn't stupid. A stretched-out zone tends to put some families far from the school building; worse shape and longer trips often arrive together, because both come from the same geography. Shape can work like a smoke alarm: it can't see fire, but it often goes off when there is one. The catch is that a smoke alarm can also stay silent during a fire, or go off over burnt toast. The 2023 consolidation produced a clean example of all three:

① The alarm works — Spiritridge. Absorbing the Eastgate territory stretched the zone badly: its shape score fell from 0.581 to 0.314, the biggest drop in the district. And this time the weird shape really did come with a human cost: the zone's average drive rose from 4.0 to 7.5 minutes — a 19-minute walk becoming a 41-minute walk. Shape flagged it, and the flag was right.

② The alarm stays silent — Woodridge. Woodridge's score is identical before and after: 0.340 to 0.340. Not one meter of its boundary moved. Every shape metric ever invented says nothing happened here. Meanwhile its building went from 56% to 100% full and its distance to Advanced Learning collapsed from 5.1 km to 1.5 km, because the program moved in. A zone was transformed, and shape couldn't see it — the same blindness that hides the middle- and high-school ripple, where no line moved but the entering classes changed.

③ The alarm cries wolf — the shoreline. Medina scores 0.361, Clyde Hill 0.299, Newport Heights 0.277 — among the "worst" shapes in the district, in part because their zones trace Lake Washington's coves and points. A boundary that follows a lake, a highway, or a neighborhood edge looks exactly like a boundary someone gerrymandered — and following a neighborhood edge is the community-preserving choice that the continuity card praises. Here the score punishes the innocent explanation.

Today's shape scores, worst to best. Dark teal = zones tracing Lake Washington; light teal = zones touching Lake Sammamish. Bennett, the lowest scorer of all, borders Lake Sammamish — the other lake drags scores down too. And Spiritridge cuts the other way: only a sliver of its boundary is shoreline, and its low score comes mostly from the 2023 stretch (exhibit ①), not the water. Water explains a lot here — but never all of it, in either direction.

The quiet fourth case is most of the district: eleven zones where nothing moved and the alarm correctly stayed silent.

Who absorbed the change

Every fairness argument about a boundary change is, underneath, a distribution argument: not "did costs exist?" but "who paid them?" This section lays that distribution out — and to keep ourselves honest, it follows a rule set before we looked at any results: every piece of moved territory gets the same fixed set of numbers, chosen in advance. Here are the facts on both sides, side by side. Fueling the concern that parents raised in news coverage1,9 — that the plan "favored wealthier communities" — is the top of the table: the Wilburton area, with some of the district's densest housing and one of its largest concentrations of limited-English households (the language card), was closed and folded into the two zones below it, which ranked among the district's most expensive by income and home value. Cutting the other way is what the arrows show: the receiving zones' medians dropped or held when the merge happened, the merged zones became demographically more mixed, not less (the mix-gap card), and the heaviest travel burden landed on the Eastgate side, whose profile matches the district almost exactly. Both columns of facts are true at once. We report the concern as the community's stated concern, and the numbers as numbers.

One background pressure belongs in this picture without being attributable to it: housing costs. Families in the denser, more affordable pockets of the district were already under market pressure before any line moved, and nothing in our data can separate "moved because of the rezoning" from "moved because of rent." We state it as context, not as an effect.

Whether this distribution was acceptable is not a math question. The rezoning's costs were real, they were not spread evenly, and the community that absorbed the most of one cost (time) was not the community the loudest concern was about (income). That tension is exactly the kind of thing the affected public — not this website — gets to weigh.

Section 6

What the numbers can't say

This site measures a lot: routes, seats, shapes, shares. Ending there would quietly imply that what got measured is what mattered — and that implication would be the least honest thing on the site. So this section names what our numbers cannot reach, plainly, before handing the judgment over.

The process itself. The largest unmeasured question is the one the participation card holds open: not whether families could speak — the meetings were public and uniform — but whether speaking worked, and worked equally, for families with less time, less English, and less practice with school politics. News coverage reported concerns about a fast timeline and confusing communication;1,8 checking those concerns properly means reading the procedural record — board minutes, comment logs, translation availability — which is evidence to weigh, not a number to map. On this site, that question is deliberately kept as a question.

Things that were never on a map. Even with perfect data, a boundary analysis cannot see:

  • What a route feels like. Our minutes count travel; they don't know which crossings a seven-year-old shouldn't make alone, or which stretch has no sidewalk.
  • What a school means. A closure doesn't just reassign students; it dissolves a PTA, teacher relationships, morning rituals — the stability card counts rezonings, not what a rezoning takes.
  • Whether the money worked. The district projected savings; auditing whether the consolidation delivered them, and what the savings bought, is a budget question outside our data.
  • Trust. How a decision was experienced shapes how its outcomes are received. No layer measures that, and pretending otherwise is how "data-driven" becomes a way of not listening.

One caution about the words "not measurable": they do not mean less important, and they certainly do not mean deniable. Some of the most consequential parts of this story live in this section precisely because no one can put a number on them. The measured impacts set the table; they do not finish the meal.

So here is the hand-off. This site was built on one premise: the people affected by a decision deserve to judge it with the facts in hand. You now have every fact we could compute, every caveat we owed you, and the tools to check both. The judgment is yours.
