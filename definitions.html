Definitions & references

What the words mean, and how big the numbers are

Every term this site uses, in plain language — and a yardstick for every scale, so you never have to guess whether 0.03 is a lot.

Part 1

Schools & programs

Attendance zone
The area of homes assigned to a school: live inside the line, and that school is yours by right. Elementary, middle, and high school each have their own set of zones, and the lines do not have to match.
Attendance-area school vs. choice school
An attendance-area school serves its zone. A choice school has no zone at all — families anywhere in the district apply to it. Bellevue's choice schools include Jing Mei (Mandarin immersion elementary), Puesta del Sol (Spanish immersion elementary), International School, and Big Picture. Choice schools are excluded from zone-based measures on this site, because they have no boundary to measure.
Feeder pattern
Which elementary schools' students "flow up" into which middle school, and middle into high. Because the three levels' lines don't match, an elementary cohort can split between two middle schools — e.g., Phantom Lake's children divide roughly 54/46 between Odle and Tillicum.
Advanced Learning (AL Centered)
The district's accelerated academic program for students who qualify by testing. It is hosted at specific elementary schools (currently Cherry Crest, Medina, Somerset, and Woodridge) — qualifying students travel to a host school. Before 2023, Spiritridge was a host; the program moved to Woodridge in the consolidation.
Olympic program
A site-based special-education program serving students on the autism spectrum, with specialized staffing and classrooms. Hosted at Bennett and (since 2023) Spiritridge; before 2023 its second site was Eastgate.
Pacific program
A site-based special-education program for students with developmental and intellectual disabilities. Hosted at Newport Heights and Sherwood Forest.
Cascade program
A site-based special-education program supporting students with social, emotional, and behavioral needs. Hosted at Woodridge.
Inclusion / Integrated Preschool
Special-education support that lives at many schools rather than a few sites: inclusion support classrooms, and preschool classes that mix children with and without disabilities (ages 3–5). Because they are widespread, this site lists them per school rather than measuring distance to them.
IEP
Individualized Education Program — the legal document that defines the services a student with a disability receives. Placement in programs like Olympic, Pacific, or Cascade happens through the IEP process; the district assigns the site.
Dual-language immersion
Programs teaching a full curriculum in two languages. In Bellevue: Mandarin (Jing Mei, whole school), Spanish (Puesta del Sol, whole school; plus strands within Lake Hills, Sherwood Forest, and Stevenson), and Japanese (a strand at Bennett). Ardmore and Newport Heights additionally host Arabic and Korean language-support programs.
IB (International Baccalaureate)
A rigorous high-school diploma program; in Bellevue it is hosted only at Interlake High School.
Limited-English household
The census definition: a household where no one aged 14 or older speaks English "very well." These are the households for whom school communication — letters, meetings, deadlines — is hardest to navigate.

Part 2

The measures

Polsby–Popper (shape score)
Compares a zone's area to its perimeter. A perfect circle scores 1; the stringier the outline, the closer to 0. It measures only the outline — no people, prices, or travel times are in the formula.
Reock, convex-hull ratio, minimum-rectangle ratio, moment of inertia
Four sibling shape scores, each comparing the zone to a different "ideal" shape (smallest enclosing circle, rubber-band outline, tightest rectangle, spread around the center). They exist because no single shape score is trustworthy alone — the map tool shows all of them per zone.
Utilization
Enrollment divided by building capacity. 100% = exactly as many students as seats.
Mix gap
Our per-zone measure of demographic distinctiveness: how far a zone's mix of residents (six groups: White, Asian, Hispanic, Black, two-or-more, other) sits from the district's overall mix. 0 = identical to the district.
Dissimilarity index (D)
A standard segregation measure. Intuition: out of every 100 children, how many would have to switch schools for every school to mirror the district's mix. 0 = every school already mirrors the district; 100 = total separation.
Theil's H (entropy index)
A second standard segregation measure that handles many groups at once and can be decomposed. 0 = no sorting; 1 = complete sorting. We report it alongside D because relying on a single index is exactly the mistake this site warns about.
The frozen population
Every era on this site uses the same underlying residents: race and age come from the 2020 census (block level), and income, home values, rents, and language come from the ACS 2019–2023 survey (block-group level, split across zones by where people live). Real families moved during these years; we deliberately hold the data still so that differences between eras isolate what the line-drawing alone did. Enrollment counts are the one exception — those are real per-year headcounts.
Road distance & travel time
Computed on the real road network (not straight lines), for driving and walking separately. Times are typical, traffic-free estimates.

Part 3

How big is big? Yardsticks for every scale

Shape scores (Polsby–Popper)
Bellevue's elementary zones run from about 0.25 to 0.65. Nothing here is near the "perfect" 1.0 — real zones never are. A change of 0.1 is large; a drop of 0.25 (Spiritridge, 2023) is the biggest in the district's recent history.
Mix gap
The scale in practice: 0 = identical to the district; 0.07 (Stevenson) = the most distinctive zone in Bellevue. So 0.01 is "nearly indistinguishable," 0.03 is "middling," and anything near 0.06 is "one of the most distinctive zones in the district."
Dissimilarity index
Bellevue's elementary zones today: about 15 of every 100 children would have to move to equalize residence-based mixes, 19 of 100 based on actual enrollment. For context, values above 60 are what researchers historically called "hypersegregation" in large metros — Bellevue's numbers are low by national standards, which is itself worth knowing.
Distances
Rules of thumb: 1 km ≈ a 12-minute walk for an adult (slower with a six-year-old); within Bellevue, driving averages roughly 2 minutes per km without traffic. The site's access thresholds: within 3 km, within a 10-minute drive, within a 20-minute walk.
Walking times
A useful anchor: about 20 minutes is the commonly used upper end of a reasonable elementary walk. 40+ minutes each way effectively means the trip will be driven or bused, every day.
Utilization
~55% = nearly half the building empty (Wilburton before closure); ~95–100% = full but fitting; over 100% = more students than seats (Spiritridge before 2023, at 110%). Whether ~100% is "efficient" or "no room to breathe" is a judgment the number doesn't make.
Money (ACS 2019–2023 figures)
Bellevue's elementary-zone medians run from about $124k to $201k household income, $0.8M to $1.8M home value (all but two zones above $1M), and $2,050 to $3,130 monthly rent. For scale, the U.S. median household income in this period was about $75k — every zone in this district sits far above it, which is the backdrop for every "wealthier communities" argument on this site.
Children counts
The district has about 23,300 school-age children (5–17) in the 2020 snapshot. So the ~2,835 children in territory reassigned in 2023 ≈ 12% of the district's school-age children.

Every yardstick here uses this district's own data (2020 census, district enrollment counts) except the U.S. median income and the walking-speed rule of thumb, which are general references. Full sourcing will live in Methods & Sources.
