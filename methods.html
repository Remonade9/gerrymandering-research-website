Methods & sources

How every number on this site was made

The data, the formulas, the design choices, and the limits — so any claim here can be checked, and any skeptic can retrace our steps.

Section 1

The design: a real before and after

Studies of boundary fairness usually face a hard question: fair compared to what? Researchers answer it by inventing a counterfactual — a "what-would-it-look-like-otherwise" map to compare against, such as assigning every home to its nearest school, or simulating thousands of computer-drawn maps. The invented baseline is always debatable, and studies using different baselines reach different conclusions.1,2

This project largely sidesteps that debate, because Bellevue's history supplies a real comparison: the district redrew its elementary boundaries in 2018 and again in 2023. The map before each change is the baseline. Every measure on this site is computed three times — once per boundary era — and compared.

The frozen population

All three eras use the same residents: the 2020 census. Real families moved during these years, but if each era used its own population, a difference between eras could mean "the lines changed" or "the people changed," with no way to tell them apart. Holding the population still isolates what the line-drawing alone did.

Why 2020 specifically

The 2020 decennial census is the only complete, block-by-block count available — year-by-year alternatives exist only as survey estimates for much coarser areas. The cost of this choice is described honestly in Limitations.

School enrollment is the deliberate exception: those are real per-year headcounts, because "how full was the building" is a question about a specific year.

Section 2

Boundary data, era by era

Only elementary boundaries changed in 2018 and 2023; middle- and high-school zones are identical in all three eras (itself a checked finding, not an assumption).

  • 2023–now ("after"): current attendance-area boundaries from the City of Bellevue's open GIS data.
  • 2018–2023 ("before"): recovered from an archived City of Bellevue GIS layer and validated geometrically: the recovered Wilburton zone's 8.50 km² equals, to the hundredth, the territory Clyde Hill (+3.60) and Enatai (+4.90) gained in 2023, and ten of the era's sixteen zones are geometrically identical to today's — exactly the zones untouched by the consolidation.
  • Pre-2018: the federal School Attendance Boundary Survey (SABS, 2015–16), a different source drawn at a different precision. We clip these zones to the district boundary (the raw versions run into Lake Washington, which would corrupt shape scores) and treat their exact edges as approximate throughout the site.

How moved territory is computed

The Boundary-changes page computes moved territory directly: the before-map and after-map are overlaid, and every area whose assigned school differs becomes a "piece," dissolved per from→to flow. Near-zero slivers along shared edges (boundary-snapping noise, with no residents) are filtered; for the pre-2018 comparison, a larger filter absorbs the cross-source imprecision. Piece totals were validated against the independently known zone-size changes above.

Program locations, by era

Program locations (Advanced Learning, dual-language, site-based special education) are era-specific: the 2018–2023 hosting list comes from the per-school table in the district's own consolidation report (October 2022 enrollment snapshot), current hosting from district program pages and the 2024–25 substitute handbook. Pre-2018 program locations are too thinly documented in public sources to support distance measures, so the pre-2018 era shows none — a distance to the "nearest host" computed from an incomplete host list would overstate distances, and a wrong number is worse than no number.

Section 3

People data

Race and age — 2020 census blocks

Race and age come from the 2020 census at the block level (the smallest census unit; 1,327 blocks in the district): race/ethnicity from the redistricting file (P.L. 94-171), age counts from the demographic profile (table P12). Each block is assigned to the zone containing its location point, per era; blocks are small enough that this assignment is essentially exact.

Income, rents, and language — ACS block groups

Income, home values, rents, and language come from the American Community Survey (ACS) 2019–2023 five-year estimates (tables B19013, B25077, B25064, C16002), published for block groups — areas much larger than blocks, which sometimes straddle zone boundaries. A straddling block group's counts are split across zones in proportion to its 2020 block population on each side (population apportionment); zone medians are population-weighted averages of the block-group medians. A "limited-English household" follows the census definition: no member aged 14+ speaks English very well.

Enrollment and capacity

Enrollment comes from NCES Common Core of Data per-school counts (plus the district's own October snapshots where cited); building capacity from district facilities documents (2023 consolidation report for elementaries; 2019 assessments for secondary schools — vintages are stated wherever they mix). One counting rule applied everywhere: NCES totals include preschoolers, the district's K-5 counts don't, so any quoted enrollment states whose count it is.

Travel

Travel is computed on the real road network: for every block, the driving and walking distance and time to each relevant school were precomputed with the openrouteservice engine (based on OpenStreetMap). Times are typical, traffic-free estimates.

The written record — how the participation account was built

The process story (the participation card, the District overview record, and the quotes across the Analysis page) is built from documents rather than computation: news coverage of the 2023 consolidation from multiple independent outlets, and the district's own records — the consolidation report, official schedules and updates, the superintendent's published Q&A transcripts, and the seven PTAs' joint letter. Many of the district's original web pages were later removed, so citations point to archived copies. Three rules govern how that record is used: every claim is attributed to the person or document that made it; where accounts conflict (as with the Eastgate communication dispute), both sides are presented; and events in sequence are never presented as cause and effect unless a source states the connection itself.

What population does each number describe?

Read every figure with its base in mind:

  • School-age children (5–17, 2020 census) — all travel and program-distance figures are averaged over where children live; zone "kids" counts and the neighborhood split counts use the same base.
  • All residents (2020 census) — race shares, the mix gap, and the residential segregation indices.
  • Households (ACS) — income, home value, rent, and limited-English shares.
  • Enrolled students (per year) — enrollment, capacity, utilization, and the enrollment-based segregation indices (which also exclude choice schools, since they have no zone).

Section 4

The measures

Shape (compactness) scores — per zone, per era

  • Polsby–Popper = 4πA / P², where A = area and P = perimeter. A circle scores 1.
  • Reock = A / area of the smallest circle enclosing the zone.
  • Convex-hull ratio = A / area of the zone's convex hull (the rubber-band outline).
  • Minimum-rectangle ratio = A / area of the smallest rotated rectangle containing the zone.
  • Moment of inertia: the geometric (area-only) version — how spread the zone's area is around its centroid, normalized so a circle scores 1; computed in closed form from the polygon.

A note on how the measuring is done: raw map coordinates are latitude and longitude — angles, not distances — so before measuring anything we convert to a flat map "projection" whose coordinates are honest meters (UTM zone 10N, designed for our part of the globe, where its distortion is negligible). Which projection you pick can silently move compactness scores,6 so ours is stated. All five scores are computed in that projection, and each zone also gets a percentile rank among its own era's zones. Five scores are shown, not one, because no single shape score is trustworthy alone — scores can disagree, and implementation choices (projection, resolution, coastline handling) move them.6,7

Travel & program access — per zone, per era

For each zone: the average distance/time from children's homes to the assigned school (and to the nearest host of each program), weighted by where children live, plus minimum, quartiles, median, maximum, and shares within thresholds (3 km; 10-minute drive; 20-minute walk). The distribution curves in the side panels come from discrete data — census blocks — so the smooth-looking curve is really a cumulative count: for each distance x, the share of children living within x, sampled at forty points between zero and the zone's maximum. Both straight-line and road-network versions exist; the site displays road values wherever routing is available.

Capacity — per school, per era

Utilization = enrollment / building capacity, from the era's own headcount against the best-documented capacity for that period.

Demographic measures

  • Race shares — a zone's percentage in each group (six groups: non-Hispanic White, Asian, Black, two-or-more, other; Hispanic of any race), from 2020 blocks.
  • Mix gap — per zone: compare the zone's share of each of the six groups to the district's share of that group, and add up how far off they are, with bigger weight on the groups that make up more of the zone. Zero means the zone's mix is identical to the district's; the district-wide index is the child-weighted average across zones. (For the mathematically inclined: it is the Kullback–Leibler divergence of the zone's mix from the district's — the same information-theory family as Theil's H; see Reardon & Firebaugh (2002)4 for that family of measures.)
  • Dissimilarity index (D) — district-wide, White vs. non-White: the share of one group that would need to switch schools for every school to have a perfectly even mix. 0 = already even; 1 = totally separate. The full mathematics is in Duncan & Duncan (1955).3
  • Theil's H (entropy index) — district-wide, all six groups: how much less diverse the average school is than the district as a whole. 0 = every school mirrors the district; 1 = complete separation. Its formula is genuinely involved, so we don't reproduce it here — the definition and its justification are in Reardon & Firebaugh (2002).4 H is used because it is the only multigroup index that both respects transfers and decomposes cleanly — a reasoned choice, not a convention. Both D and H are computed on two bases: who lives in each zone (2020 blocks) and who enrolls at each zoned school (NCES 2023–24).
  • Neighborhood splits — for each official City of Bellevue neighborhood, the number of zones its school-age children divide across, per era; portions outside the district are reported separately and never counted as an extra school.
  • Stability — how many times each area's assignment changed across the three eras (zero, once, or twice), from the boundary overlays in Section 2.

Section 5

Where this sits in the literature

The school-zone lineage

The closest published work compares real attendance zones to invented baselines: Richards measured 15,290 zones against nearest-school ("Voronoi") versions and found irregular boundaries slightly increase segregation on net;1 Monarrez, with a later dataset, a different index, and a decomposition separating school siting from boundary drawing, found boundaries slightly integrate (about −5% relative to the distance-minimizing baseline, with roughly 86% of boundary-level differences carrying through to actual enrollment).2 Two careful studies, opposite signs — the answer depends on the index, sample, and baseline chosen. Both agree that effects vary sharply district by district, which is precisely why a single district's real before/after event is worth studying on its own terms. Richards and Stroub also showed that zone irregularity can be an "accident of geography" rather than evidence of manipulation5 — the argument our Analysis page's shape section develops with Bellevue's lakes.

The cautions from redistricting mathematics

That literature supplies the rules this site is built around. Compactness scores can be moved by implementation choices alone and a map can pass every shape test while engineered underneath;6 optimizing compactness does not produce fair maps — across states the two are essentially uncorrelated;7 single gerrymandering metrics can be gamed and sometimes mislead outright;8 different metrics frequently disagree about the same map;9 and geography itself — coastlines, mountains, where communities actually sit — produces irregular, innocent boundaries that formulas alone cannot tell from engineered ones.10 Hence this site's rules: many measures, no composite score, no verdicts.

Why no simulated ensembles

The strongest modern redistricting analyses compare an enacted map against thousands of simulated alternatives.11,12 Ensembles answer "is this map unusual?" — an attribution question. Our question is "what did this change do?", and for that the before-map is the natural comparison; a simulated pile of hypothetical Bellevues would add machinery without adding an answer.

Section 6

Limitations

First and always: this site does not judge fairness. No metric here is a fairness score, and none of the numbers ranks any school, neighborhood, or decision as right or wrong. Within that stance, the honest limits of the data:

  • One frozen population. Everything demographic rests on a single snapshot (2020 census; ACS 2019–2023 for household measures). That isolates boundary effects cleanly, but it means the pre-2018 era is described with people who lived there in 2020, not 2016 — and none of the eras captures demographic change over time.
  • No behavioral response. People react to rezonings — switching to private school, transferring, moving house, prices adjusting. None of that feedback is modeled; our numbers show what the lines did to a fixed world, not how the world answered back.
  • Pre-2018 geometry is approximate (cross-source, federal SABS); its shape scores and moved-territory pieces are close estimates, not parcel-exact.
  • Capacity vintages mix where noted (2023 elementary values, 2019 secondary); choice-school capacities are older still, so their pins show enrollment only.
  • Distance is reach, not access. Program-distance measures answer "can you get there," never eligibility, admission, or available seats; special-education placement is assigned through the IEP process, not chosen by distance.
  • Enrollment ≠ residence. Choice schools draw district-wide; about 982 students (2023–24) commute in from outside the district; some residents attend private schools or other districts. Zone-based measures describe residents.
  • Within-district scope. A large share of regional school segregation lies between districts;2 boundary changes inside one district can only ever touch the within-district share.
  • Travel times are traffic-free estimates on today's road network, applied to all eras.
  • Index choice matters. Segregation and compactness values depend on which groups are counted and which formula is used3,4 — which is why the site shows several measures and invites you to compare them rather than trust any one.
